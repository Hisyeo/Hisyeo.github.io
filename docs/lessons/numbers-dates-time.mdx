# Numbers, Dates, & Time

We use numbers everywhere for common tasks such as giving directions,
determining quantities, and navigating our day. In Hisyëö even the concept of
having multiple of something is considered apart of the same category of
numbers. This category is called "determiners" and some determiners are
quantities and others are concepts related to numbers.

## Cardinal Numbers

us exactly how many of something we have. The table below provides the numbers

## Tens, Thousands, Millions

As you can see above, you use the cın with the number directly following
it representing how many groups of 3 places. This is the same amount as we put
commas or periods in our number representations.

## Quantifying Things

If you supply a number on the left-hand side of any noun phrase (in the
determiner position), it effects whether the noun is seen as plural or singular.
These are called **Cardinal Numbers**. You can supply any amount of number words
here and they follow the rules shown above to generate their value or amount.

<HisyeoTabs>
  <p>düi viyën son hövezi "two hundred and three cows"</p>
</HisyeoTabs>

If you specify only the number 1 (kut), it acts like the indefinite
article ("a/an" in English) but this is usually omitted.

<HisyeoTabs>
  <p>kut ënpukun "one burp" or "a burp"</p>
</HisyeoTabs>

The language allows you to omit any sense of plurality and this frees you from
having to worry about how to properly pluralize words. In many languages, the
plurality of the object is often then replicated in related words. In Hisyëö
there is no need for any of that and typically you will setup some context of
what a particular object is referring to and then you just use the head noun to
refer back to that prior definition including the plurality established.

## Which Place?

If you supply a number on the right-hand side of any noun phrase (in the
modifier position), it indicates which specific value in a list of values, also
known as the *placement*. These are called **Ordinal Numbers**.

communicate dates and time as well.

## Navigating The Calendar

Telling people on what day, week or month something has or will occur is an
extremely important tool in any language. Without a calendar system, how can we
schedule a doctor's appointment, go out on a date, or find out when our favorite
show returns?

### Days of the Week

The Hisyëö week days are, unlike the rest of the calendar, named after the most
prevalent color of the planets of our solar system as seen in true color
photography of the planet. The start of the week was chosen as Monday to
coincide with the near majority of the world agreement on this and it allows
Saturday and Sunday to be the "Weekend" (könyëc/ɔ̃ıɀ̄ʇ
wëxön/ʒʇɋ̃ı).

In order to indicate a specific day use one of the demonstrative pronouns
(övo/ɽıɤ, ulyö/ɽ͊ʃɀı, onö/ɽƨı). Otherwise, a weekday
(or really any use of lono) is either one or many unspecified days. You
can use a day as a condition in a hön prepositional phrase:

<HisyeoTabs>
  <p>hön xokön lono xolxili, ü nü lı si lö pı xopëk müt motüo sënzö</p>
  <em>"Every Sunday, we used to go to grandpa's"</em>
</HisyeoTabs>

Asking what day of the week it is can be done by asking what color of day:

<HisyeoTabs>
  <p>lono övo lı longı zık. <em>"What day is today?"</em></p>
</HisyeoTabs>

To say something is "in the summer" or similar, supply one of these as the
object of a hoi preposition.

### Exact Dates

Specifying an exact date involves using ordinals for the day, the week day
terms, and ordinals for the months. When specifying what date a weekday has, a
relative clause is used to treat the following phrase as an
[appositive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apposition).

<HisyeoTabs>
  <p>lono övo lı lono kut yë wuto son. <em>"Today is the March 1st"</em></p>
  <p>lono lözic niün lı lono mihüon do lı lono zıyön yë wuto tiü. <em>"His birthday is Tuesday, May 14th"</em></p>
</HisyeoTabs>

### Saying Years

Years are said like two segments of 2 numbers. For example, 1931 is "19" and
"31"; 384 is "3" and "84". If the most significant segment is under 10, you can
use xünyu preceding the final year digit. If you are referring to a
recent date, you can often just mention the final segment.

<HisyeoTabs>
  <p>dus tiso xëti dus xëti <em>1977</em></p>
  <p>son xünyu son <em>303</em></p>
</HisyeoTabs>
      
To say dates in "BC" or "BCE", use the phrase yë ılık zızıme sodolun.

<HisyeoTabs>
  <p>köson si sınsi Lömo hoi
     xëti tiü dus son yë
     ılık zızıme sodolun. <em>Rome was built in 753 BCE.</em></p>
</HisyeoTabs>

When you are setting up a date or appointment, often times you want to provide which weekday
in addition to the date.

<HisyeoTabs>
  <p>noyo
     hoi lono zingu do lı lono kinsë yë wuto son,
     lı cok müt nıwos.
     <em>Friday, the 15th of March, I am departing for home.</em></p>
</HisyeoTabs>

Similar to English, the appositive "the 15th of March" must be closed by a comma
to return to the main sentence clause for the remaining consituents.

## Talk The Clock

If you know how to count to 12, you can talk about hours; if you know how to
count to 60, then you can talk about minutes and seconds.

To ask the time, you use the copula with a null subject. The complement uses the
interrogative pronoun, so there is no need for a sentence ender.

<HisyeoTabs>
  <p>woküt zık <em>"What time is it?"</em></p>
  <p>suhot zık <em>"What hour is it?"</em></p>
</HisyeoTabs>

That's a complete sentence because the verb is actually a base noun being used
as a verb which is interpreted as a [zero
copula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_copula) sentence. The subject in this
sentence is not provided, these are called
[null-subject](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null-subject_language) sentences.
Whenever this happens, you have to interpret the subject from either the current
sentence or from preceding sentences. In some cases, this ends up being "it" or
"there" in English. In other cases, it ends up being the prior sentence's
subject. Here's some examples of how to respond to these questions:

<HisyeoTabs>
  <p>kut <em>"It is one"</em></p>
  <p>suhot kut <em>"It is one o'clock"</em></p>
  <p>suhot dus <em>"It is ten o'clock"</em></p>
  <p>suhot pole <em>"It is twelve o'clock"</em></p>
  <p>lono mutyu <em>"It is noon"</em></p>
  <p>pıtün mutyu <em>"It is midnight"</em></p>
</HisyeoTabs>

Hisyëö uses the 12-hour clock to reduce the need for multiple word ordinals to
create times like "23:00". Often times, you can deduce which half of the day
from context. If you need to use the equivalent of "a.m." or "p.m":

<HisyeoTabs>
  <p>suhot tiso yë ılık lono mutyu <em>"9 o'clock before noon"</em></p>
  <p>suhot tiü yë cıhöü lono mutyu <em>"9 o'clock after noon"</em></p>
</HisyeoTabs>

For some specific times with importance, here's how to translate:

<HisyeoTabs>
  <p>woküt kon socok lono <em>"morning time"</em></p>
  <p>woküt yë cöüvon kut <em>"breakfast time"</em></p>
  <p>woküt yë cöüvon mutyu <em>"lunch time"</em></p>
  <p>woküt yë cöüvon vëlcin <em>"dinner time"</em></p>
  <p>woküt yë küon nëös <em>"dawn"</em></p>
  <p>woküt yë küon könyëc <em>"dusk"</em></p>
  <p>woküt kon conzodo <em>"bedtime"</em></p>
</HisyeoTabs>

Now you can say many things about the time of day or describe when things occur.
In order to give any sentence a time that it occurred, we use the hoi
preposition.

<HisyeoTabs>
  <p>könyëc ü woküt yë cöüvon
     kut hoi tukwos <em>"Breakfast is ending at 8"</em></p>
</HisyeoTabs>

### Durations

If you need to say an amount of time rather than a point in time, you follow the
normal approach for creating plurality: provide the number words in the
determiner slot (left side of the head word).

<HisyeoTabs>
  <p>yünyoü ü nimü cö son suhot <em>"You have three hours"</em></p>
  <p>puswen ü tüu ulyö cö kut suhot <em>"That task needs an hour"</em></p>
</HisyeoTabs>

Most of the time when we are referring to time, we think of it as a specific
location on the flow of time (hence why it shares the same preposition particle
as we use for denoting the location of an action). When you need to refer to
a duration/length of time we use a different particle meant for distances and
durations.

<HisyeoTabs>
  <p>si tüu ü ni lon nonkü suhot <em>"We did that for four hours"</em></p>
</HisyeoTabs>

### Exact Times

If you need to talk about specific times within an hour, there is a base
modifier content word that does double duty as a time unit for minutes.

<HisyeoTabs>
  <p>gö somö lon tiü ëvun <em>"It will only be 5 minutes"</em></p>
</HisyeoTabs>

## Measurements

To indicate a measurement of something, it helps to have a way to describe a
"unit" of measurement. There is no exact word for this in Hisyëö, although
<HisyeoText kind='latin'>cëö</HisyeoText> and
<HisyeoText kind='latin'>yogö</HisyeoText> have their uses which we'll discuss
below. The word "unit" means "singular entity" which suggests that we should
reach for the number one (<HisyeoText kind='latin'>kut</HisyeoText>). To help
add some clarity, let's use the agentive verb noun particle to create a meaning
of "one-er" or something whose state is primarily about oneness.

<HisyeoTabs>
  <p>gö yë öni kut yë di volo möi et yë zomi ü hondon. <em>"The family is the basic unit of society."</em></p>
  <p>ondo ü küngiyo cö do ëo vunlit ölë cızi müt düi yodo son öni kut. <em>"The company plans to split into two or three units."</em></p>
</HisyeoTabs>

Other than the time words, the rest of the measurement units are done by just
referring to what kind of measurement the unit is for.

### Temperature

<HisyeoTabs>
  <p>socok ü ximu cö vıldonpun lon kut viyën cëö yë tüt. <em>"Water boils at 100 degrees Celsius."</em></p>
</HisyeoTabs>

### Mass/Volume/Weight

For mass, assume that measurements are in grams or pounds (depending on where
you are in the world). Add <HisyeoText kind='latin'>cënpö</HisyeoText> for kilograms or tons.

> It is widely recommended that adults consume 15-35 grams of fiber daily, although many Americans fall short.
> 
> —Casey Seiden, Verywell Health, 3 May 2024

<HisyeoTabs>
  <p>pësnëhot nulto cö vos mokon ü umo dës xonluk gıtno dus tiü moliyü son dus tiü öni kut yë küsowi cö zöhün vünië dës siüvo hoi xokön lono lëkın püzök onö ü mikdol yë ëto umo Umelıkın</p>
</HisyeoTabs>

### Length/Width/Height

For mass, assume that measurements are in meters or feet (depending on where you
are in the world). Add <HisyeoText kind='latin'>cënpö</HisyeoText> for kilometers or miles.
